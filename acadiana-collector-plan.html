Plan B — Acadiana Tire Collector / Hauler

The "live where you want" option · Prepared for William · June 2026

One-line thesis: Acadiana is the worst place to start a competing processor — Colt, Inc. (Scott) already processes ~3 million tires/year and is a top-20 US processor. But it's a great place to be a collector/transporter that feeds Colt: far lower capital, no head-to-head fight, and you get to live in the region you prefer.
Capital range: ~$60k (one truck + boxes, used) to ~$220k (multi-truck).
Trade-off: lower ceiling than owning a processor, but dramatically lower risk and capital.
Why this fits you. You said Acadiana is where you'd prefer to live. You don't have to give that up to be in the tire business — you just shouldn't try to out-process Colt on its home turf. Become its supplier instead. Collectors are the front end of the whole system, and Colt's scale means it always needs feedstock.

1. The market reality in Acadiana

  • Colt, Inc. (Scott, LA) — ~3M scrap tires/year, two permitted sites (Scott + New Orleans), statewide dumpsite remediation. Dominant. Opening a competing processor next door means worse economies of scale on day one against the incumbent.
  • The opening is upstream, not downstream. Every tire Colt processes has to be collected and hauled first. Dealers, fleets, parishes, and dumpsite cleanups all need a reliable transporter. That's a fragmented, relationship-driven niche — exactly where a small local operator competes well.

2. The business model: registered waste-tire transporter

Contract with tire dealers, auto shops, fleets, and parish collection events to pick up scrap tires on a schedule, then deliver them to Colt (or another permitted processor). Revenue = pickup/tip fees charged to generators, minus your fuel/labor, with the tires delivered into Colt's gate.

Key advantage: a transporter generally needs LDEQ registration and manifesting — but not the heavy processor permit, financial assurance at processor scale, fire plans, or pile permits. The regulatory and capital burden is a fraction of Plan A.

Confirm the licensing tier with LDEQ. Louisiana regulates waste-tire generators, transporters, and processors differently. Verify the exact transporter registration, manifest, and any bonding requirements with Dutch Donlon (225-219-3388) before buying a truck — this plan assumes the lighter transporter tier, which must be confirmed.

5. Execution roadmap

Phase 0 — Validate (weeks 1–4)

  1. Call Colt, Inc. (337-235-0353, teresa@coltscraptire.com) — ask whether they buy/accept delivered tires from independent transporters and on what terms.
  2. Confirm the transporter registration tier and manifest rules with LDEQ (Donlon).
  3. Survey Lafayette-area dealers/shops on current pickup reliability and what they'd pay.

Phase 1 — Set up (months 2–3)

  1. Register as a waste-tire transporter; set up manifesting and recordkeeping.
  2. Buy one used truck + boxes; bind commercial insurance.
  3. Sign 8–15 recurring dealer/shop accounts to anchor a route.

Phase 2 — Grow (months 4–12)

  1. Densify routes; add fleet and parish-collection-event contracts.
  2. Bid on dumpsite cleanup hauling (Colt and parishes both generate this work).
  3. Add a second truck once routes justify it.

6. How this connects to Plan A

A collector/hauler is the natural on-ramp to the NE-LA processor: it teaches the generator relationships, the logistics, and the LDEQ paperwork at a fraction of the capital. If the NE-LA gap (Franklin Rubber) confirms as open, the same trucks and accounts can feed your own plant instead of Colt's. You can run this in Acadiana now and convert to Plan A later — or keep both.
