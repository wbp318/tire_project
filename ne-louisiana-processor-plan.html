Plan A — Northeast Louisiana Waste Tire Processor

Primary recommendation · Prepared for William, East Carroll Parish, LA · June 2026

One-line thesis: Northeast Louisiana's only tire processor (Franklin Rubber, Winnsboro, ~60–70 mi away) is failing under fire-marshal and town orders. If that gap stays open, a clean, rurally-sited, end-market-anchored processor can capture NE-LA tire volume that otherwise long-hauls south — and earn the state's per-pound subsidy.
Capital range: ~$330k (lean, used equipment + contract hauling) to ~$870k (comfortable).
Make-or-break factor: a confirmed LDEQ-approved end market (buyer) before any equipment purchase.
⚠️ All dollar figures are planning ranges. Confirm with equipment quotes, an insurance agent, a surety-bond agent, and LDEQ before relying on them. The "$35M tire plant" figure online is for a full crumb-rubber/pyrolysis operation — not this model.

1. Why this is the recommended location

  • The gap is real and reopening. Per LDEQ's Jan 2025 list, the only NE-LA processor is Franklin Rubber Resources (Winnsboro), which has had 14 fire-department responses since 2010, a State Fire Marshal cease-and-desist since Sept 23, 2023, a town vacate order, and repeated closures. The next-nearest processors are Benson (Sibley, ~150 mi) and Colt (Scott/Lafayette, ~210 mi).
  • Closest to home — no relocation from East Carroll Parish required.
  • End market is nearby: the Graphic Packaging paper mill in West Monroe (~70 mi) is the region's largest industrial fuel user and the primary tire-derived-fuel (TDF) target.
  • State tailwinds: LDEQ held 2024 town halls in West Monroe/Shreveport over NE underservice, launched a 2025 parish cleanup pilot, and statute R.S. 30:2411(B) directs the state to favor in-state recycling businesses.
Learn from Franklin Rubber's mistakes — they ARE the business plan. Their failure came from (1) an in-town industrial lot surrounded by neighbors, (2) uncontrolled tire piles that caught fire repeatedly, and (3) margins too thin to fund cleanup. Your plan inverts all three: rural land you control, disciplined pile limits + a fire plan the FD signs, and a guaranteed buyer so tires move through the site instead of piling up.

2. The business model: "shred-and-haul"

Collect tires → shred to chips with steel/wire separation → haul TDF chips to a paper mill or cement kiln and sell recovered steel to a scrap outlet. This is the lowest-capital path and matches the 800–1,500 tires/month demand LDEQ flagged. (Higher-value crumb rubber or pyrolysis are later upgrades, not a starting point.)

The three revenue streams — you need all three

  1. Tip fees charged to generators/parishes — ~$1.50–2.00/passenger tire, $8–20+/large (confirm).
  2. State subsidy — minimum 7.5¢/lb (~$150/ton) on material reaching an LDEQ-approved end use (LAC 33:VII.10535). A supplement, not full-cost coverage; in-state tires only.
  3. End-product sales — TDF chips and recovered steel.

5. Revenue & the honest break-even

Assumes ~22 lb per passenger-tire-equivalent (PTE); subsidy = 7.5¢/lb = ~$1.65/PTE (~$150/ton).

Stream1,500 tires/mo (~16.5 t)3,000 tires/mo (~33 t)

Gross monthly revenue~$6,200–7,700~$12,500–15,500
Monthly operating cost~$8,000–17,500 (labor, fuel/maintenance, freight, insurance/bond/admin)
The honest read: At 1,500 tires/mo you are break-even to slightly negative — too small to carry fixed costs. At ~3,000+ tires/mo it turns modestly profitable, and improves with volume because most costs are fixed. Viability = volume. You must aggregate tires from multiple NE parishes (East Carroll, Madison, Tensas, Richland, Franklin, Ouachita, Morehouse) and keep the haul to your buyer short.

6. Execution roadmap

Phase 0 — Validate (weeks 1–6, ~$0)

  1. Call Dutch (Lewis) Donlon, LDEQ — (225) 219-3388, Lewis.Donlon@la.gov. Ask: (a) current status of Franklin Rubber Resources — is the NE gap open? (b) does LDEQ see room for an additional NE processor? (c) which end markets are approvable from NE LA? (Draft email already prepared.)
  2. Call Graphic Packaging, West Monroe — ask the fuel/environmental manager whether they accept or would pilot TDF, and to what spec (chip size, wire content, moisture). This drives equipment choice.
  3. Gauge supply — call NE parish police juries and tire dealers about current tire volumes and disposal pain.

Phase 1 — Secure the end market & site (months 2–5)

  1. Get at least one letter of intent from a buyer (ideally Graphic Packaging) — de-risks everything and strengthens the permit.
  2. Let the buyer's spec (de-wired chips vs. whole) decide your equipment — not the other way around.
  3. Identify a rural site you control with the 100-ft buffer built in. Avoid in-town lots (Franklin Rubber's fatal error).
  4. Line up the local fire department for the required FD agreement early.

Phase 2 — Permit & finance (months 4–10)

  1. Engage an environmental engineer to prepare the permit application, fire plan, and vector plan.
  2. Arrange financial assurance (surety bond) and commercial insurance.
  3. Ask LDEQ/EPA about recycling grants — the 2024 town-hall report repeatedly raised grant funding for tire recycling.
  4. Submit permit application; work the public-notice process.

Phase 3 — Build & launch (months 8–14)

  1. Site prep, install scale, take delivery of (used) shredder + separation + loader.
  2. Sign supply contracts with parishes/dealers before opening so volume is there on day one.
  3. Launch lean; reinvest toward the 3,000+ tires/mo threshold; only then consider crumb-rubber upgrade.
