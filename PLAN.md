## 1. The one rule that drives everything

**Whole scrap tires are banned from Louisiana landfills.** They must go to a registered
tire dealer, a permitted **waste-tire processor**, or an authorized parish collection
point. So a landfill is usually *not* your answer for whole tires.

Louisiana runs a **Waste Tire Program** through the Department of Environmental Quality
(LDEQ). It's funded by a fee you already paid when you bought tires (roughly **$2.25**
per passenger/light-truck tire, **~$5** per medium/commercial truck tire, and **~$10**
per off-road/large tire — *confirm current amounts*). Because that fee is prepaid:

- Many **tire dealers will take back scrap tires** (free or a small fee).
- **Processors are paid by the state** to recycle them.

**Upshot:** your cheapest legal path is almost always a **tire dealer or a parish
collection point**, not a landfill.

## 2. Tier 1 — the easy ~30 passenger & farm-truck tires

Cheapest and closest first:

1. **East Carroll Parish Police Jury / parish solid-waste office (Lake Providence).**
   *Make this your first call.* Ask:
   - "Does the parish run a scrap-tire collection day or have a designated drop-off?"
   - Parishes get state grants for periodic **tire amnesty/cleanup events** — these are
     often **FREE** for residents. If one is coming up, that alone could clear most of
     your pile at no cost.

2. **Local tire shops / auto shops** (Lake Providence, Tallulah ~25 mi, Monroe/West
   Monroe ~70 mi). Under the state program many accept scrap tires. Ask:
   "Do you take scrap tires for disposal, and what's the per-tire fee?"
   **Typical: $2–4 per passenger tire.**

3. **Walmart Auto Care Center** — nearest are **Tallulah, LA** and **Lake Village, AR
   (~12 mi)**. Many locations take old tires for disposal (~**$1.50–3 each**) even if
   you didn't buy there — but it's store-dependent, so **call the specific store first**.

## 3. Tier 2 — the tractor/ag tires (the costly, tricky part)

Big ag tires are a different problem: **most tire shops, Walmart, and even some
landfills won't take them**, or they charge **by weight**. Budget **$15–60+ each**.

Where they *can* go — call ahead before driving:

- **The dealer that sells tractor tires** nearest you (Lake Providence / Tallulah /
  Monroe). They handle old casings and will often take yours, sometimes **free with a
  new-tire purchase**.
- **A permitted waste-tire processor that handles OTR/ag tires** — most likely found in
  the **Monroe** area (largest nearby city) or one of the extended hubs (Section 5).
  Ask LDEQ for the nearest one (Section 6).
- **The parish/regional landfill** — ask specifically: "Do you accept large ag tires,
  and is it priced per tire or by weight?"

**Money-savers for ag tires:**
- **Cut them** in halves/quarters with a reciprocating saw — reduces volume and
  sometimes the fee.
- **Remove the rims** — avoids "tire-on-rim" surcharges.
- **Sort by type** so you only pay ag rates on the ag tires, not the whole pile.

## 4. Free / get-paid angles (be realistic)

**The honest truth:** scrap/unusable tires almost always **cost money** to offload.
Getting *paid* is rare — buyers want clean, gradeable casings or big volume, not a
mixed junk pile. So don't count on income; count on **avoiding cost**. *(The one "cash"
idea — cutting truck-tire sidewalls to sell as silage-cover rings to outfits like
24tons or KSI Supply — isn't worth the labor for one ~40-tire pile, and they don't buy
whole tires from the public anyway.)*

The cheapest "disposal" is **free reuse**, and your area is ideal for it: roughly **74%
of East Carroll Parish is farmland**, so the cattle/hay crowd that actually uses these
tires is right next door. Best plays, roughly in order:

- **Tractor-tire water troughs & feed bunks** — *the best home for your big tractor
  tires.* One large tire becomes a near-indestructible, freeze-resistant cattle water
  trough (cut one sidewall, seal the bottom) or a feed bunk. Ranchers seek these out
  because they're free/cheap — let the rancher do the cutting.
- **Silage / hay-tarp weights** — whole car and truck tires are the classic weight laid
  over plastic to hold down silage piles, baleage, and hay tarps; one producer can
  absorb dozens. ⚠️ **Climate caveat:** whole tires hold water and breed mosquitoes
  (West Nile), and Louisiana limits on-farm tire stockpiles — so pitch them for **active
  use laid flat on tarps**, not stacked storage.
- **Raised garden beds / retaining & erosion walls** — soaks up odd large tires the
  cattle crowd skips; gardeners take smaller passenger tires for planters and tree rings.

**How to reach the demand:**
1. **Post two separate free-pickup ads** on Facebook Marketplace (set location to Monroe
   to cover NE LA): one *"Free used tires — you haul"* for passenger/farm-truck tires
   (clears in days), and a separate *"Free tractor & ag tires, you load & haul — great
   for silage weights, troughs, feed bunks"* so the right buyer self-selects. Bump every
   few days; allow ~2 weeks for the biggest tires. Whole tractor tires go **free, not for
   cash.**
2. **Local parish buy/sell/trade & farm groups** — search Facebook for *Parish Buy Sell
   Trade*, *Tallulah / Lake Providence yard sale*, *Delta Cattlemen*, *NELA farm
   equipment*. Local members mean low haul cost for them.
3. **LSU AgCenter — East Carroll / Madison Parish Extension office.** The parish
   livestock/ag agent personally knows local cattle and hay producers and can connect
   you to someone who wants tarp-weight or trough tires. Main AgCenter line
   **225-578-4161** *(HQ switchboard — ask to be routed to your parish office)*, or use
   the "Find Your Agent" page at lsuagcenter.com.

## 8. Do / Don't

**Do**
- Call ahead every time — confirm they take your tire *types* and the fee.
- Sort tires by type before loading so you pay ag rates only on ag tires.
- Keep disposal receipts (proof of legal disposal).
- Snap a photo and post the tractor tires "free, you haul" before paying to dump them.

**Don't**
- **Don't dump or burn them.** Illegal-dumping and open-burning penalties in Louisiana
  are steep, and tire piles are mosquito- and fire-hazards.
- Don't haul big ag tires anywhere without calling first — a wasted trip with a loaded
  trailer is the most common mistake.
