Plan C — Arkansas Opportunity

Tires & general waste, across the state line · Prepared for William · June 2026

One-line thesis: Arkansas's statewide tire program is government-run and broke (ran out of money in 2022; privatization debated for 2025). That could open private contracts — but the underlying economics are even weaker than Louisiana's, so treat Arkansas as optional upside, not a foundation. The "general trash" angle you raised is a much larger, capital-heavy, monopoly-held business; the realistic small-business entry there is hauling, not processing.
Why it's on the table: East Carroll Parish is on the AR line (Lake Village ~12 mi).

Part A — Arkansas waste tires

How Arkansas runs tires (very different from Louisiana)

  • No per-pound subsidy to private processors. Instead, the Tire Accountability Program (TAP) runs through regional Used Tire Programs (UTPs) operated by the Solid Waste Management Districts.
  • Funded by a $3 rim/tire fee; after deductions only ~$2.31/tire remains to reimburse UTPs — the stated break-even point, not profit.
  • Districts contract haulers/processors. Near you: the Southeast Arkansas Regional Solid Waste Management District (SEARSWMD, "myseagreen") — free drop-off (4 tires/person/month, then $2.90/tire), hauling via contractor. Delta collection centers: DeWitt, Warren, Rison, Tillar-Winchester, Sheridan, White Hall.

The problem = the opportunity (and the catch)

  • The program ran out of money in August 2022; tires piled up. The Legislature has repeatedly declined to raise the fee, so revenue doesn't cover rising costs.
  • A UTP's contractor became "operationally inconsistent"; in June 2025 a UTP sought to raise its reimbursement from $2.90 → $5.01/tire just to keep a processor willing to operate.
  • The program's architect (Rep. Lanny Fite) pushed to dismantle and privatize it; privatization legislation was expected in 2025.
The catch: the reason the program is failing is structural — there isn't enough money in the fee to make processing profitable. Privatization doesn't fix that; it shifts the risk to the operator. Officials themselves warned privatization means "less service and more expense." Don't build a plant for the Arkansas program.
How to play it (low-risk, optionality):
  1. Treat AR Delta generators near the line (Lake Village, McGehee, Dermott) as incremental supply for a Louisiana-based operation (Plan A or B). Note: AR tires almost certainly won't earn the LA subsidy (in-state rule) — they'd be tip-fee/end-market revenue only. Confirm with LDEQ.
  2. Get on the contact list now with ADEQ's Tire Accountability Program and SEARSWMD so you hear about any RFPs/contracts or privatization early. If AR opens processor contracts to the private market, that's the trigger to re-evaluate.

Part B — General trash / MSW (the bigger, harder business)

Honest assessment, because you asked: "trash processing" is not a close cousin of tire processing.

  • Landfills, transfer stations, and material-recovery facilities (MRFs) are capital-intensive (often $10M+), heavily permitted, long lead-time, and largely controlled by national haulers (Waste Connections, Republic, WM) and the regional Solid Waste Management Districts that hold the permits. This is a utility-style business, not a small-business entry.
  • The realistic small-business entry into "trash" is collection/hauling: residential/commercial roll-off dumpsters, construction debris (C&D), junk removal. New entrants are actively expanding in Arkansas (e.g., a central-AR dumpster company growing in 2025), which signals room in the hauling layer.
  • The useful overlap: a roll-off / hauling business can carry both scrap tires and C&D/junk on the same trucks and boxes — one fleet, multiple revenue streams. That's the practical synergy, rather than trying to be an MSW processor.
Read: if the goal is "a money opportunity near home with manageable capital," a dual-stream hauling business (tires + roll-off debris) straddling the LA/AR line is more achievable than an MSW processing facility — and it pairs naturally with Plan B's collector model.

Next steps on the Arkansas track

  1. Contact ADEQ Tire Accountability Program (adeq.state.ar.us/tap) and SEARSWMD — ask: (a) current processor/hauler contract status, (b) whether privatization is moving in the 2025 session, (c) how a private hauler/processor gets on their bid list.
  2. Confirm with LDEQ (Donlon) whether out-of-state (AR) tires can count toward the Louisiana subsidy (almost certainly no — but it decides whether AR volume is worth hauling).
  3. If pursuing general-waste income, price a roll-off / junk-hauling startup (trucks + boxes + a disposal relationship with an existing landfill) rather than a processing facility.
